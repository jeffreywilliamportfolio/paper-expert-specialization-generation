\begin{abstract}
A widely cited reading of mixture-of-experts (MoE) language models holds that
their experts do not specialize by subject matter: routing measured over supplied
text spreads each expert across many topics, with chemistry, law, and the rest
sharing the same few high-frequency experts. We show that this reading rests on
half of the forward pass. A transformer routes tokens in two regimes: a parallel
prefill pass that reads the prompt, and a step-by-step generation pass that writes
the answer, and supplied-text routing is the prefill half. Across a suite of
prompts spanning twenty academic domains, we identify the expert that receives the
most routing weight in each domain, separately for each pass. Over prefill a
single high-frequency expert wins almost every domain and routing looks
domain-blind, reproducing the familiar negative picture. Over generation the
winners disperse and most domains acquire their own leading expert. The shift is
large and one-directional, survives a control that pads all prompts to a common
length, and reproduces in a second, larger model whose expert indices are entirely
different while the prefill-concentrated, generation-dispersed pattern holds. The
upshot is a correction to how prior negative results should be read: prefill is the
half the field measured, generation is the half it missed, and domain
specialization in these models is largely a property of the generation pass.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} mixture-of-experts; expert specialization; routing;
prefill; generation; autoregressive decoding; interpretability.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Mixture-of-experts (MoE) layers replace a dense feed-forward block with many
parallel expert blocks and a router that, for each token, selects a few of them.
Only the selected experts run, so the model can hold a large parameter count
while keeping per-token compute small. Because the router makes a discrete,
inspectable choice at every token, it invites a natural question: do the experts
carve knowledge up by subject, so that one becomes the chemistry expert and
another the law expert? The most-cited look came up empty.
\citet{jiang2024mixtral}, analyzing Mixtral's routing on validation text,
``do not observe obvious patterns in the assignment of experts based on the
topic''; the structure they find is syntactic and positional. That negative result
is load-bearing: it is widely read as evidence that MoE experts simply do not
specialize by domain.

That look, and most like it, scored routing over supplied text. A transformer
processes a sequence in two distinct regimes. During \emph{prefill}, the whole
prompt is processed in parallel to populate the key/value cache. During
\emph{generation}, output tokens are produced one at a time, each attending to
everything before it. Supplied-text routing is the prefill regime. Routing happens
in both passes, and the two are free to disagree. They disagree in a specific,
consistent way, and that disagreement decides whether domain specialization is
visible.

We route a domain-balanced prompt suite through two MoE models and, for each of
twenty domains, identify the expert that receives the most routing weight,
once over prefill positions and once over generated positions. Over prefill, one
generalist expert wins $18$ of $20$ domains in the smaller model and $13$ of $20$
in the larger one, and routing looks domain-blind, reproducing the familiar
picture. Over generation, the winners disperse: nearly every domain is won by its
own distinct expert, and the generalist recedes. The same expert that looked like
``the answer to everything'' while the model read the prompt gives way to
per-domain leaders once it begins to write.

What each expert encodes stays outside our scope, and we do not re-measure Mixtral.
The result is about \emph{where} in the forward process domain structure becomes
measurable: prefill is the half the field measured, and generation is the half it
missed. The canonical negative result holds for the prefill half it measured; it has
been read too broadly, as a verdict on specialization, because supplied-text routing
is domain-blind by construction of where it looks.

This paper makes three contributions. (1)~We draw a methodological correction:
reading expert specialization off prefill or supplied-text routing, or off routing
pooled across passes, systematically understates it, which reframes how the field's
negative results on MoE specialization should be read. (2)~We establish the effect
that drives the correction: separating routing by forward-pass regime, prefill
versus generation, places domain specialization in the generation pass and shows it
largely absent from prefill, measured as the concentration of per-domain expert
winners. (3)~We show the effect survives a length-matched control that equalizes
token counts across the two passes, and that it reproduces in a second, larger model
whose expert indices do not correspond to the first.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Mixture-of-experts routing.}
The mixture-of-experts construction routes inputs to specialized subnetworks
through a learned gate \citep{jacobs1991adaptive}. \citet{shazeer2017outrageously}
made this sparse and scalable for deep networks with a top-$k$ gate over thousands
of experts, and the design was carried into large transformers by Switch
Transformers \citep{fedus2022switch} and ST-MoE \citep{zoph2022stmoe}. The models
we study are open MoE checkpoints in the Qwen3 lineage \citep{yang2025qwen3}.
Across this line of work the router exposes an inspectable choice at every token,
tempting a reading of it as a map of internal organization.

\paragraph{Do experts specialize by topic?}
This question is the paper's starting point, and the most-cited answer is negative.
\citet{jiang2024mixtral} analyze Mixtral's routing on validation text and report
that they ``do not observe obvious patterns in the assignment of experts based on
the topic'': the expert distribution is similar across arXiv, biomedical, and
philosophy documents, with structure instead in syntax and token position. The
statement is carefully scoped to what it measured --- routing over supplied text,
which is the prefill regime --- and it has since hardened, in looser uptake, into
the broader belief that MoE experts do not specialize by domain.
\citet{dai2024deepseekmoe} reach a compatible intuition from the engineering side:
they treat strong specialization as something to build in, through fine-grained
expert segmentation and shared-expert isolation, which presumes that ordinary
top-$k$ routing specializes weakly. Our measurement extends the first result and
qualifies the second. Read over prefill we, too, see little domain structure; the
same probe read over the generation pass resolves into per-domain experts. The
delta to \citet{jiang2024mixtral} is the axis of measurement: the forward-pass
regime.

\paragraph{Input- and token-dependent routing.}
Recent work shows routing depends strongly on the input.
\citet{mynampati2026taskconditioned} construct ``routing signatures'' and recover
task identity from routing alone with high accuracy, establishing that the router
is task-sensitive; and \citet{hayashi2026layerwise}, working with the same
Qwen3.5-35B-A3B checkpoint we use, find that routing during code generation is
strongly token-identity dependent and layer-dependent. Both condition on \emph{what}
is being processed (task or token), pooled within a regime; neither contrasts the
prefill and generation passes for the same prompts, which is the comparison we make.

\paragraph{Prefill versus generation.}
The two-pass structure of transformer inference (a parallel prefill over the prompt
followed by step-by-step decoding) is well established on the systems side, where the
two phases have distinct compute and latency profiles and are increasingly served
separately \citep{kwon2023pagedattention,zhong2024distserve}. We use the same
distinction representationally: the two passes route to different experts, and the
difference is where domain specialization becomes measurable.

\section{Why prefill routing is a tempting place to look}
\label{sec:whyprefill}

There are practical reasons an analyst reaches for prefill routing first. Prefill
is deterministic: the same prompt and the same weights produce the same router
logits, with no sampling in the loop, so prefill statistics are exactly
reproducible and easy to compare across runs. Prefill is also cheap to capture: a
single forward pass over a short prompt yields one routing vector per token, no
generation required. And prefill is where the prompt's content actually
sits, so it is intuitive to expect topical routing to be strongest exactly there,
while the model is ``looking at'' the chemistry question.

The measurement below shows that this intuition is inverted. The content of the
prompt is present during prefill, but the routing over prefill positions is
dominated by a few generic experts and is largely blind to domain. The
domain-specific structure appears later, while the model is producing
domain-specific text. The reasons we can offer for this are partial
(Section~\ref{sec:interp}), but the empirical pattern is clean enough to state on
its own and to act on.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\paragraph{Probe.}
The prompt suite contains $60$ short questions covering $20$ academic domains
(archaeology, biology, chemistry, comparative religion, computer science,
cybersecurity, economics, environmental science, history, law, linguistics,
mathematics, medicine, neuroscience, philosophy, physics, political science,
psychology, software engineering, statistics), with $3$ questions per domain. Each
question is a plain, domain-typical prompt (for example, a request to contrast two
core concepts in the field).

\paragraph{Models.}
We use two MoE checkpoints from the same Qwen3.5 lineage
\citep{yang2025qwen3}, both 8-bit quantized fine-tunes (HauhauCS ``uncensored''
variants): a 35B-parameter model with $\approx
3$B active per token, and a 122B-parameter model with $\approx 10$B active. Both
route each token to the top $8$ of $256$ experts per MoE layer. The two models
have different layer counts and different internal layouts, so their expert
indices are not comparable to each other; an expert numbered $114$ in one model
has nothing to do with expert $114$ in the other. Decoding is greedy. Generation
is capped (at $2{,}048$--$2{,}056$ new tokens); we return to the consequences of
that cap in Section~\ref{sec:limits}.

\paragraph{Routing reconstruction.}
For each token we take the router's pre-softmax logits over all $256$ experts,
apply softmax, keep the top $8$, and renormalize over the selected experts so the
eight weights sum to one. For an expert $e$ on a fixed set of tokens (one pass of
one prompt at one layer) we form three reductions of these renormalized weights:
$S_e$, the fraction of tokens on which $e$ is selected; $Q_e$, its mean weight on
the tokens where it is selected; and $W_e$, its mean weight over all tokens (zero
where unselected). Because all three reduce the same weights, $W_e = S_e\,Q_e$
holds exactly on any single token set; we use this only as a bookkeeping check that
the selection mask, the conditional mean, and the marginal mean are mutually
consistent, and find $\max_e\lvert W_e - S_e Q_e\rvert \le 5.6\times10^{-17}$
across all capture cells. The identity is not expected to survive averaging across
prompts: where a per-domain or overall $W$, $S$, and $Q$ is reported (in the
accompanying tables, not in this paper), each is a separate mean over the domain's
prompts and the model's layers, so the tabulated $S$ and $Q$ are marginal means
whose product differs from the tabulated $W$ by the across-prompt $S$--$Q$
covariance. Domain winners are ranked by $W$, the mean routed weight per token,
which is well defined regardless of the decomposition.

\paragraph{The two passes.}
For each prompt we partition its token positions into the prefill block (the
prompt tokens) and the generation block (the tokens the model produced). All
routing statistics are computed within one block or the other, never pooled
across them. For each domain we then form the per-domain mean of $W_e$ over that
domain's three prompts and call the highest-$W$ expert the \emph{domain winner}.
This yields, per model and per pass, a list of $20$ domain winners. The analysis
in this paper is almost entirely about how those two lists differ.

\paragraph{Concentration of a winner list.}
To summarize a list of $20$ winners we use three numbers. The \emph{distinct
count} is how many different experts appear. \emph{Max wins} is the number of
domains taken by the single most frequent winner. And the normalized entropy of
the winner distribution, $H = -\sum_i p_i \log_2 p_i / \log_2 20$ where $p_i$ is
the fraction of domains won by expert~$i$, runs from $0$ (one expert wins
everything) to $1$ (every domain has its own winner). These are deterministic
summaries of the winner lists; they introduce no new model evaluation.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Over prefill, one generalist wins almost everything}

In the 35B model, the prefill domain-winner list is dominated by a single expert
that takes $18$ of the $20$ domains. Only two domains escape it: chemistry and
linguistics each have their own prefill winner. The same expert is also the top
expert overall on the prefill block. By the summaries of Section~\ref{sec:methods}, the prefill
winner list has just $3$ distinct experts and a normalized entropy of $0.13$
(Table~\ref{tab:conc}). Read literally, the prefill routing assigns one expert to
nearly every subject: a single high-frequency default standing in for the rest.

\subsection{Over generation, the winners disperse}

The generation domain-winner list for the same model and the same prompts diverges
sharply from its prefill list. All $20$ domains are won by $20$ distinct experts;
no expert wins more than one domain. The normalized entropy of the winner list
rises from $0.13$ to $1.00$. The generalist that won $18$ domains during prefill
wins exactly one during generation (political science) and otherwise drops out of
the per-domain lead. In its place, domain-specific experts surface. In philosophy,
for instance, the generation winner held only rank~$5$ within philosophy during
prefill ($W = 0.0188$ generation versus a mid-pack prefill weight), and it wins no
other domain.

The dispersion holds regardless of where we cut the generated text. These models
do not emit a clean end-of-turn token; they spell out the end-of-turn marker as
literal text and then continue into hallucinated follow-up turns until the token
cap. Trimming the generation block at that marker, or leaving it untrimmed,
produces the same $20$ distinct domain winners. The structure sits in the answer
content.

\subsection{Quantifying the shift}

Table~\ref{tab:conc} collects the concentration summaries for both passes and both
models, and Figure~\ref{fig:winners} shows the two leading numbers visually. The
pattern is the same in both models and large in both: a concentrated, low-entropy
winner list over prefill becomes a dispersed, high-entropy one over generation.
The generalist's reach (max wins) falls from $18$ to $1$ in the 35B model and from
$13$ to $2$ in the 122B model.

\subsection{Token count does not explain the shift}

A first objection is that prefill and generation differ in length: a prompt is
tens of tokens, an answer is hundreds, and longer token sets could disperse the
top experts for purely statistical reasons. We address this with a token-balanced
design. Three prompts are each packed with twenty domain questions and padded to
exactly $446$ prompt tokens, and each is allowed $2{,}048$ generated tokens, so
the prefill and generation blocks are matched in scale and the per-chunk
comparisons use identical token budgets.

Within this design the prefill and generation \emph{expert sets} barely overlap.
Figure~\ref{fig:balanced} reports the Jaccard overlap between the top experts of
the two passes for each packed chunk: by routed weight it is $0.00$, $0.23$, and
$0.07$ across the three chunks, and by selection rate $0.07$, $0.23$, and $0.00$.
With token counts held fixed, the set of experts that dominates generation is
mostly disjoint from the set that dominates prefill. The routing entropy itself is
almost unchanged between the two passes ($0.958$ prefill, $0.953$ generation):
generation spreads each token over a similar number of experts and routes them to
\emph{different} ones. The regime moves the winners; length holds steady.

\subsection{The pattern reproduces in a second model with different experts}

The 122B model gives an independent replication. Its expert indices are unrelated
to the 35B model's, and indeed a different generalist emerges: over prefill, one
expert wins $13$ of $20$ domains (with a second taking $2$ more), for $7$ distinct
winners and entropy $0.42$, less extreme than the 35B model and still strongly
concentrated. Over generation the winners disperse to $18$ distinct experts across
the $20$ domains, entropy $0.95$ (Table~\ref{tab:conc}, Figure~\ref{fig:winners}).
The global top experts also change identity between passes: the prefill leaders
are one set of indices and the generation leaders are a different set. The specific
experts change from model to model; the prefill-concentrated, generation-dispersed
shape carries over.

The two models also illustrate that an expert's role can move between passes
within a single model. In the 122B model the expert that leads philosophy
\emph{generation} is mid-pack during philosophy prefill, while the expert that
happened to look philosophy-relevant in the smaller model's generation has, in the
larger model, its only strong footprint in a different domain (computer science).
We read these as cautions against naming experts from any single pass or carrying
an index across models.

\section{Interpretation}
\label{sec:interp}

The measurement supports a narrow, mechanical claim. In these models the routing
over prompt positions is dominated by a few high-frequency experts and carries
little domain signal, while the routing over generated positions resolves into
distinct per-domain leaders. Domain specialization, to the extent the router
expresses it at all, is largely a property of the generation pass.

A plausible reading of the prefill behavior gives the field's negative results a
principled cause. Reading a short prompt is a fairly generic operation (tokenizing,
parsing, building context) for which the router falls back on common, broadly useful
experts; domain-specific computation is recruited once the model commits to producing
domain-specific content token by token. On that reading, supplied-text routing is
bound to miss domain structure, because it observes the model only during the pass
that has yet to recruit the domain machinery. This is a hypothesis about the cause.
The data locate the effect; its cause stays open.

A deflationary reading would credit the dispersion to the generation pass in
general: perhaps generation simply routes more diffusely, and the spread of winners
follows from that. Two entropies, measured together in the token-balanced control,
tell against it. The per-token routing entropy holds nearly steady across passes
($0.958$ prefill, $0.953$ generation): the model spreads each token's weight over a
similar number of experts in both regimes. The winner-distribution entropy moves
far, from $0.13$ to $1.00$ in the 35B model and from $0.42$ to $0.95$ in the 122B
model. The per-token spread stays put while the identity of each domain's leading
expert changes. The generation pass relabels the domain leaders, and that
relabeling is the specialization signal.

The implication corrects the reading the field has drawn. An analysis that
characterizes expert--topic structure from prefill routing, or from routing pooled
over a sequence without separating the passes, sees the concentrated prefill picture
and understates specialization. The same probe,
read over the generation pass, recovers per-domain structure. So a negative result
obtained over supplied text reports the prefill half accurately and leaves the
generation half unmeasured; when the goal is to ask what an MoE's experts
specialize in, the generation pass is the place to measure, and the two passes
should be reported separately.

\section{Limitations and falsification tests}
\label{sec:limits}

\paragraph{What the present evidence does not yet settle.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Cross-family scope is the boundary of the sharp claim.} Both
checkpoints are 8-bit quantized HauhauCS fine-tunes of Qwen3.5 MoE models. The
pattern reproduces across two sizes with non-corresponding indices, which carries
the model-independent, method-level reading: supplied-text routing understates
specialization. Turning that reading into a statement about any one prior result
requires re-measuring that result's setup. Concretely, re-running the Mixtral
routing analysis of \citet{jiang2024mixtral} over the generation pass would upgrade
the corrective from inference to demonstration; we predict it would resolve the
topic structure their supplied-text analysis did not show. Until such a test, the
demonstrated claim covers this lineage and the correction applies to the
measurement convention.
\item \textbf{Generation length and the token cap.} Generated blocks are long and
frequently reach the cap (in the 122B run, $49$ of $60$ cells hit $2{,}048$ tokens;
mean generated length $\approx 1{,}868$), and every cell eventually spills into
chat-template continuation. Trimming at the spill boundary leaves the 35B winner
list unchanged, which argues against a pure spill artifact, but a length- and
cap-matched generation budget per prompt would make the comparison cleaner.
\item \textbf{``Domain winner'' is a coarse statistic.} It reduces a domain to its
single top expert by mean weight. A domain with two near-tied leading experts and
a domain with one dominant expert are treated the same. Reporting the full
top-$k$ per domain, and the stability of the winner across the three prompts,
would give a fuller picture than the winner alone.
\item \textbf{Significance is asserted descriptively.} The concentration
summaries describe the observed winner lists; we have not attached a
randomization test that asks how often a winner list this dispersed would arise
under a null of pass-independent routing. The $18$-versus-$20$ and $13$-versus-$18$
contrasts are large, but a permutation test would quantify them.
\item \textbf{Three prompts per domain.} Twenty domains at three prompts each is
enough to expose the prefill/generation contrast but thin for per-domain claims.
The per-domain winner is the least reliable level of this analysis.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Tests that would confirm or break the claim.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Re-run the Mixtral analysis of \citet{jiang2024mixtral} --- the same data and
the same routing readout --- over generated tokens as well as supplied text. The
corrective predicts that the topic structure absent over supplied text resolves over
generation; this is the direct test of the correction and the experiment that would
carry it from a method-level inference to a model-specific demonstration.
\item Run the same probe on a base (non-fine-tuned) Qwen3.5 MoE and on an MoE from
a different family (for example a different vendor's top-$k$ router); the claim
predicts the same prefill-concentrated, generation-dispersed shift with,
again, non-transferable indices.
\item Fix a common generation budget per prompt and re-measure, removing the
cap and the variable-length confound entirely.
\item Replace the single-winner statistic with a permutation test that shuffles the
prefill/generation labels on token blocks and asks how often the dispersion gap
seen here arises by chance; a corrective aimed at the field's negative results
invites this stricter standard of evidence.
\item Increase prompts per domain (say $10$--$20$) and report winner stability, so
per-domain assignments carry a confidence; the higher-stakes framing makes that
per-domain reliability worth the additional captures.
\item Track, position by position within the generation block, when a domain's
generation winner overtakes the prefill generalist; this would test the
``specialization is recruited as content is produced'' reading directly.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conc}

Two MoE language models route prompt tokens and generated tokens to different
experts, and the difference is structured. Over the prompt-reading pass a single
generic expert wins almost every domain; over the generation pass the domains
acquire distinct leading experts. The shift is large, survives matching prompts to
a common length, and reproduces across two model sizes whose expert indices do not
correspond. The lesson is a correction. The widely cited negative result on MoE
expert specialization was measured over supplied text, the prefill half of the
forward pass, the obvious and cheap place to look, and the half where the structure
stays hidden. Prefill is the half the field measured; generation is the half it
missed, and the half where specialization resolves. For anyone asking what an MoE's
experts specialize in, separate the two passes and read specialization from
generation.

\section*{Reproducibility and audit note}

The numbers in this paper are transcribed from per-domain winner tables in an
existing capture archive and are listed claim-by-claim, with source files, in the
accompanying \texttt{SOURCES.md}. The three figures and the concentration
summaries in Table~\ref{tab:conc} are produced by \texttt{make\_figures.py} from
the winner lists alone; that script re-runs no model and performs only the
deterministic counting described in Section~3. The underlying captures were
produced with greedy decoding and a top-$8$-of-$256$ routing reconstruction whose
$W = S\cdot Q$ identity holds per token set at machine precision (residual $\le
5.6\times10^{-17}$); we use the reconstructed, log-derived routing tables rather
than any earlier hand-summarized figures. Two points of provenance are worth
stating plainly: the second model's analysis was framed in its source notes around
locating an analog of one particular expert, and we have deliberately set that
framing aside and report only the pass-dependent concentration that the same
tables support; and the per-domain winner is, as Section~\ref{sec:limits} notes, the least
reliable layer of the analysis, so the claims we rest on are the
aggregate concentration contrasts, not any single domain's assignment.
