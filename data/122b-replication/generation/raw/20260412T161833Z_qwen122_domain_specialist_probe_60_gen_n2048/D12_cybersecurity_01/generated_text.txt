Public key cryptography (also known as asymmetric cryptography) is a system that uses a pair of mathematically linked keys to secure communication. Unlike symmetric cryptography, where the same key is used for both locking and unlocking, public key cryptography uses two distinct keys: a **Public Key** (shared openly) and a **Private Key** (kept secret).

Here is a breakdown of how it works across the three main pillars you mentioned: **Key Exchange**, **Signatures**, and **Trust**.

---

### 1. The Core Mechanism: The Key Pair
Before diving into applications, it helps to understand the relationship between the keys:
*   **The Public Key**: Can be distributed to anyone. It is used to **encrypt** data intended for the owner or to **verify** a signature made by the owner.
*   **The Private Key**: Must be kept secret by the owner. It is used to **decrypt** data sent to the owner or to **create** a digital signature.

**The Golden Rule**: Data encrypted with the Public Key can *only* be decrypted by the corresponding Private Key. Conversely, a signature created with the Private Key can *only* be verified by the corresponding Public Key.

---

### 2. Key Exchange (Confidentiality)
Key exchange is the process of securely sharing a secret (usually a symmetric session key) between two parties over an insecure channel. While the term "Key Exchange" often refers to the **Diffie-Hellman** algorithm, in the context of general public key usage (like TLS/SSL), it usually refers to **Encryption**.

**How it works:**
1.  **Alice** wants to send a secret message to **Bob**.
2.  Alice retrieves **Bob's Public Key** (which is available to everyone).
3.  Alice uses Bob's Public Key to **encrypt** her message.
4.  The encrypted message travels over the internet. Even if an eavesdropper (Eve) intercepts it, she cannot read it because she doesn't have Bob's Private Key.
5.  **Bob** receives the message and uses his **Private Key** to **decrypt** it.

**Why use this?**
Public key algorithms (like RSA or Elliptic Curve) are computationally expensive. Therefore, in modern systems (like HTTPS), public key cryptography is often used just to exchange a temporary **symmetric key** (like AES). Once the symmetric key is exchanged, the rest of the session uses the faster symmetric encryption.

---

### 3. Digital Signatures (Authenticity & Integrity)
While encryption ensures secrecy, digital signatures ensure that a message actually came from the claimed sender and hasn't been tampered with.

**How it works:**
1.  **Bob** wants to send a message to **Alice** and prove it came from him.
2.  Bob generates a **hash** (a unique digital fingerprint) of the message.
3.  Bob encrypts this hash using his **Private Key**. This encrypted hash is the **Digital Signature**.
4.  Bob sends the original message and the signature to Alice.
5.  **Alice** receives the message. She uses **Bob's Public Key** to **decrypt** the signature, revealing the original hash Bob calculated.
6.  Alice independently calculates the hash of the received message.
7.  **Verification**: If Alice's calculated hash matches the decrypted hash from the signature, she knows:
    *   **Authenticity**: Only Bob could have created that signature (since only he has his Private Key).
    *   **Integrity**: The message was not altered in transit (since the hashes match).

---

### 4. Trust (The Web of Trust and PKI)
The biggest challenge in public key cryptography is not the math, but **identity**. If Alice downloads a public key claiming to be "Bob," how does she know it actually belongs to Bob and not an imposter named "Evil Bob"?

This is solved through **Trust Models**:

#### A. Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) & Certificates
This is the most common model used on the web (HTTPS).
*   **Digital Certificates**: A public key is bundled with identity information (name, organization, expiration date) and signed by a trusted third party called a **Certificate Authority (CA)** (e.g., DigiCert, Let's Encrypt).
*   **The Chain of Trust**:
    1.  Your browser has a list of "Root CAs" pre-installed and trusted.
    2.  When you visit a website, the server sends its certificate.
    3.  Your browser checks if the certificate was signed by a Root CA it trusts.
    4.  If the signature is valid, the browser trusts that the public key inside the certificate belongs to that specific website.

#### B. Web of Trust (Decentralized)
Used famously in PGP/GPG (email encryption).
*   There is no central authority. Instead, users sign each other's keys.
*   If you trust **Charlie**, and Charlie has signed **Bob's** key, you might choose to trust Bob based on your trust in Charlie.
*   Trust is cumulative and subjective, based on how many people you trust have vouched for a specific key.

### Conclusion
Public key cryptography solves the fundamental problem of secure communication without a pre-shared secret. By separating the keys into public and private halves, it allows anyone to send you a secret message (using your public key) while allowing you to prove your identity to anyone (using your private key).
