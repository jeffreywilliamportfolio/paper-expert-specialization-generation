John Maynard Keynes (1883–1946) is widely considered the most influential economist of the 20th century. His work fundamentally reshaped how economists understand the economy and how governments manage it. Before Keynes, the dominant economic orthodoxy was **Classical Economics**, which assumed that markets were self-correcting and that the economy would naturally return to full employment.

Here is why Keynes mattered so profoundly to economics and macroeconomic policy:

### 1. Challenging the "Self-Correcting" Market
Before the Great Depression, the prevailing belief (Say's Law) was that "supply creates its own demand." Economists believed that if there was unemployment, wages would simply fall until it became profitable for businesses to hire everyone again.

Keynes argued in his seminal 1936 work, ***The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money***, that this logic was flawed during deep recessions. He posited that:
*   **Wages and prices are "sticky":** They do not fall quickly enough to clear markets.
*   **Demand drives the economy:** If consumers and businesses stop spending (due to fear or uncertainty), businesses cut production and lay off workers. This creates a downward spiral where low income leads to even lower spending.
*   **Equilibrium at less than full employment:** The economy could get stuck in a "liquidity trap" where it remains in a state of high unemployment for a long time without government intervention.

### 2. The Birth of Macroeconomics
Keynes effectively invented the field of **macroeconomics** as a distinct discipline. Before him, economics was largely micro-focused (studying individual markets). Keynes introduced the concept of **aggregate demand**—the total spending in an economy (Consumption + Investment + Government Spending + Net Exports).

He demonstrated that the sum of individual decisions could lead to a collective outcome that was suboptimal for everyone. This shift allowed economists to analyze the economy as a whole system rather than just a collection of individual parts.

### 3. Justification for Active Government Intervention
Perhaps his most lasting legacy is the justification for **fiscal policy**. Keynes argued that during a recession, the private sector might not spend enough to maintain full employment. Therefore, the government should step in to fill the gap.

*   **Counter-Cyclical Policy:** Governments should increase spending (or cut taxes) during downturns to boost aggregate demand, even if it means running a budget deficit.
*   **The Multiplier Effect:** Keynes showed that an initial injection of government spending could lead to a larger increase in national income. For example, if the government builds a road, the workers get paid, spend that money on goods, and the shopkeepers then spend that money, creating a ripple effect.

This theory provided the intellectual framework for the **New Deal** in the US and the post-WWII economic consensus in Europe, where governments accepted responsibility for managing the business cycle.

### 4. The Role of Expectations and "Animal Spirits"
Keynes introduced a psychological dimension to economics that is still relevant today. He argued that investment decisions are not always based on cold, rational calculations of future returns. Instead, they are driven by **"animal spirits"**—the spontaneous urge to action rather than inaction, driven by optimism or pessimism.

This insight helped explain why economies could swing wildly between booms and busts based on investor confidence, rather than just fundamental changes in productivity or resources.

### 5. Influence on Modern Institutions
Keynes's ideas directly shaped the architecture of the modern global financial system. At the **Bretton Woods Conference (1944)**, he played a central role in designing the institutions that would govern the post-war economy, including the **International Monetary Fund (IMF)** and the **World Bank**. His vision was to create a system that would prevent the competitive devaluations and trade wars that had exacerbated the Great Depression.

### Summary
John Maynard Keynes mattered because he changed the default setting of economic policy. He moved the world from a passive view of the economy (where the government waits for the market to fix itself) to an active view (where the government manages demand to ensure stability).

While later economists (like the Monetarists and New Classical economists) challenged specific aspects of his theories, the core Keynesian insight—that **aggregate demand determines output and employment in the short run**—remains a foundational pillar of modern macroeconomic policy. The debate between **Classical** and **Keynesian** economics represents the most fundamental divide in macroeconomic thought. While Classical economics dominated the 19th and early 20th centuries, Keynesian economics rose to prominence during the Great Depression and shaped policy for much of the 20th century.

Here are the key differences broken down by core concepts:

### 1. View on Market Flexibility and Self-Correction
*   **Classical Economics:** Believes markets are **perfectly flexible**. Prices and wages adjust quickly to changes in supply and demand. If there is unemployment, wages will fall until it becomes cheap enough for businesses to hire everyone again. The economy is always naturally moving toward **full employment**.
*   **Keynesian Economics:** Argues that prices and wages are **"sticky"** (rigid) in the short run. Wages don't fall easily due to contracts, unions, or social norms. Consequently, the economy can get stuck in a state of **underemployment equilibrium** (high unemployment) for a long time without external help.

### 2. The Role of Aggregate Demand vs. Supply
*   **Classical Economics:** Follows **Say's Law**: "Supply creates its own demand." The focus is on the production side (supply). If goods are produced, the income generated from that production will automatically be spent to buy those goods.
*   **Keynesian Economics:** Follows the principle that **Demand drives Supply**. If consumers and businesses are pessimistic and stop spending, demand falls, leading to lower production and layoffs. The economy is demand-constrained, not supply-constrained.

### 3. Government Intervention
*   **Classical Economics:** Advocates for **Laissez-faire** (hands-off) policy. The government should minimize its role, keep taxes low, and avoid interfering with the market. Deficits are seen as harmful because they "crowd out" private investment.
*   **Keynesian Economics:** Advocates for **Active Fiscal Policy**. The government should intervene to stabilize the economy. During recessions, the government should increase spending or cut taxes (even running a deficit) to boost aggregate demand. During booms, it should do the opposite to cool inflation.

### 4. Savings and Investment
*   **Classical Economics:** Views savings and investment as linked by the **interest rate**. If people save more, the supply of loanable funds increases, interest rates fall, and businesses borrow more to invest. Thus, savings automatically equal investment.
*   **Keynesian Economics:** Views savings and investment as determined by **income levels** and **expectations**. If people save more (increase the "propensity to save") but businesses don't invest due to low confidence, total income falls. This leads to the **"Paradox of Thrift"**: what is good for an individual (saving money) can be bad for the economy as a whole if everyone does it simultaneously.

### 5. Time Horizon
*   **Classical Economics:** Focuses on the **Long Run**. They believe that while short-term fluctuations happen, the economy will eventually return to its natural potential. As Keynes famously quipped, *"In the long run we are all dead,"* implying that waiting for the long run to fix a depression is too slow.
*   **Keynesian Economics:** Focuses on the **Short Run**. They argue that short-term fluctuations (business cycles) are the most critical period for policy because they determine current employment and living standards.

### Summary Comparison Table

| Feature | Classical Economics | Keynesian Economics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Driver** | Aggregate Supply | Aggregate Demand |
| **Wage/Price Flexibility** | Flexible (adjusts instantly) | Sticky (adjusts slowly) |
| **Market State** | Always at Full Employment | Can be in Underemployment |
| **Role of Government** | Passive (Laissez-faire) | Active (Fiscal Stimulus) |
| **Savings vs. Investment** | Interest rates balance them | Income levels and confidence balance them |
| **View on Deficits** | Harmful (Crowding out) | Useful tool in recessions |
| **Key Mechanism** | Say's Law (Supply creates Demand) | Multiplier Effect (Demand creates Supply) |

### Modern Context
Today, most economists use a **synthesis** of both schools.
*   **Neoclassical Synthesis:** Accepts that Keynesian logic applies in the **short run** (explaining recessions and the need for stimulus) but agrees that Classical logic holds in the **long run** (where supply-side factors like technology and labor determine growth).
*   However, the debate resurfaces during crises.
