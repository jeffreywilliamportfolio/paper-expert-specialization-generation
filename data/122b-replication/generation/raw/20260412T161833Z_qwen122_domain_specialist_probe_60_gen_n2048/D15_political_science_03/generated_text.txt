Nationalism and liberalism are two of the most influential organizing ideas in modern political systems. While they often overlap and reinforce one another (as seen in liberal democracies), they stem from different philosophical foundations and prioritize distinct values.

Here is a comparison of nationalism and liberalism across key dimensions:

### 1. Core Definition and Focus
*   **Liberalism** is primarily an **individualist** philosophy. It posits that the fundamental unit of society is the individual. Its central tenets include individual liberty, autonomy, rationality, and the protection of rights (life, liberty, property) against arbitrary power.
*   **Nationalism** is primarily a **collectivist** philosophy. It posits that the fundamental unit of society is the nation (a group of people sharing a common history, culture, language, or ethnicity). Its central tenet is that the nation should be the primary basis for political organization, often seeking self-determination or sovereignty.

### 2. Source of Legitimacy
*   **Liberalism:** Legitimacy derives from the **consent of the governed**. A government is legitimate if it protects individual rights and operates through mechanisms like free elections and the rule of law. The state is a tool to serve individuals.
*   **Nationalism:** Legitimacy derives from the **will of the nation**. A government is legitimate if it represents the unique identity and interests of the specific nation. The state is an expression of the nation's soul or destiny.

### 3. Scope and Universality
*   **Liberalism** tends to be **universalist**. Liberal principles (such as human rights or free markets) are theoretically applicable to all human beings regardless of their cultural background. A liberal might argue that a person in Japan and a person in Brazil possess the same inherent rights.
*   **Nationalism** is inherently **particularist**. It emphasizes the unique characteristics of a specific group. It draws boundaries between "us" (the nation) and "them" (foreigners or other nations). It argues that political arrangements should reflect specific local histories and cultures rather than abstract universal rules.

### 4. View on Diversity and Pluralism
*   **Liberalism** generally embraces **pluralism**. It seeks to accommodate diverse beliefs, religions, and lifestyles within a single political framework, provided they do not infringe on the rights of others. It often supports multiculturalism.
*   **Nationalism** often seeks **homogeneity** (or at least a dominant cultural narrative). While civic nationalism allows for diversity based on shared political values, ethnic nationalism often views cultural or linguistic diversity as a threat to national unity. It prioritizes social cohesion over individual difference.

### 5. Economic Organization
*   **Liberalism** is historically linked to **free-market capitalism**. It advocates for minimal state interference in the economy, free trade, and the protection of private property to maximize individual economic freedom.
*   **Nationalism** is more flexible economically but often leans toward **protectionism**. It may prioritize the economic interests of the nation over global efficiency, supporting tariffs, subsidies for domestic industries, or state-led development to ensure national self-sufficiency.

### 6. Potential Tensions and Synergies
*   **Tension:** The two can clash when the pursuit of national unity infringes on individual rights (e.g., suppressing minority languages to enforce a national language) or when liberal universalism undermines national sovereignty (e.g., international courts overriding national laws).
*   **Synergy:** Historically, they have worked together to create **Liberal Nationalism**. In this model, the nation provides the cultural "container" for a community of free individuals. The nation-state becomes the vehicle through which individuals exercise their right to self-government. Most modern Western democracies are hybrids of these two ideologies.
