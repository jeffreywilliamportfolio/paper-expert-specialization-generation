Radiocarbon dating and stratigraphy are two of the most fundamental tools in archaeology, but they serve different primary functions: **stratigraphy** establishes the *relative* sequence of events (what happened before what), while **radiocarbon dating** provides *absolute* numerical ages (how many years ago something happened).

Here is a detailed comparison of the two methods regarding their principles, strengths, limitations, and roles in archaeological interpretation.

### 1. Fundamental Principles

| Feature | Stratigraphy | Radiocarbon Dating ($^{14}$C) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Type of Chronology** | **Relative Dating** | **Absolute Dating** |
| **Core Principle** | Based on the **Law of Superposition**: In an undisturbed sequence of sedimentary layers, the oldest layers are at the bottom, and the youngest are at the top. | Based on **Radioactive Decay**: Living organisms absorb Carbon-14; upon death, it decays at a known rate (half-life of ~5,730 years). Measuring the remaining $^{14}$C reveals the time elapsed since death. |
| **Data Output** | A sequence (e.g., Layer A is older than Layer B). | A numerical date range (e.g., 3400 ± 40 BP). |
| **Time Range** | Theoretically unlimited (applies to any geological or archaeological sequence). | Limited to the last **~50,000 to 55,000 years**. |

### 2. Strengths and Applications

#### Stratigraphy
*   **Contextual Integrity:** It preserves the spatial and temporal relationship between artifacts, features, and layers. It tells the "story" of site formation.
*   **Cost and Speed:** It requires no expensive laboratory equipment; it is determined through excavation and observation.
*   **Universal Applicability:** It works for sites of any age (from the Stone Age to the Industrial Revolution) and any material type, provided there is a depositional sequence.
*   **Cross-Dating:** It allows archaeologists to correlate layers between different sites (e.g., if a specific pottery style is found in the same layer at two different sites, they are likely contemporary).

#### Radiocarbon Dating
*   **Precision:** It provides a specific calendar age (after calibration), allowing for the synchronization of events across the globe.
*   **Independence:** It does not rely on the assumption that layers are undisturbed. A single seed or bone can be dated regardless of its position in the soil.
*   **Resolution:** It can distinguish between events that happened centuries or even decades apart, which stratigraphy often cannot do if the layers are thin or mixed.

### 3. Limitations and Challenges

#### Stratigraphy
*   **Disturbance:** The method assumes layers are undisturbed. Natural processes (bioturbation by roots/animals) or human activity (pitting, digging) can mix layers, creating "inverted" sequences where younger items appear below older ones.
*   **Lack of Numerical Age:** It cannot tell you *when* an event happened, only that it happened *before* or *after* another event. Without an absolute date anchor, a sequence could be 100 years old or 10,000 years old.
*   **Time Gaps:** It is difficult to detect "hiatuses" (periods where no deposition occurred) without other evidence.

#### Radiocarbon Dating
*   **The "Old Wood" Problem:** If a sample is taken from the heartwood of a long-lived tree, the date reflects when the tree started growing, not when it was cut down or used by humans. This can result in dates that are centuries too old.
*   **Reservoir Effects:** Marine organisms or those in hard-water environments may absorb "old" carbon, making them appear older than they are unless corrected.
*   **Calibration Curve:** Raw radiocarbon years ($^{14}$C years) do not match calendar years due to fluctuations in atmospheric carbon. Dates must be calibrated against tree rings (dendrochronology) or other records, which can result in wide date ranges or multiple possible calendar dates.
*   **Material Specificity:** It only works on organic material (bone, wood, charcoal, shell). It cannot date stone tools or pottery directly (unless organic residue is present).

### 4. Synergy in Interpretation

In modern archaeology, these tools are rarely used in isolation; they are **complementary**.

1.  **Stratigraphy provides the framework:** Excavators use stratigraphy to define the context of a find. They know that a specific charcoal sample came from a hearth located in Layer 3, which sits above Layer 4.
2.  **Radiocarbon provides the anchor:** The charcoal from Layer 3 is sent for $^{14}$C dating. This assigns a numerical date to that specific layer.
3.  **Cross-Verification:** If the radiocarbon date for Layer 3 is 2000 BP, but the pottery style suggests it should be 5000 BP, the archaeologist investigates the stratigraphy for errors (e.g., was the charcoal intrusive? Was the layer disturbed?).

### Conclusion

**Stratigraphy** is the logic of the site; it explains the *sequence* and *context* of human activity. **Radiocarbon dating** is the clock; it assigns a *temporal coordinate* to that activity.

*   Use **stratigraphy** to understand the formation of the site and the relative order of cultural changes.
*   Use **radiocarbon dating** to place those changes on a global timeline and to test the validity of the stratigraphic sequence.
