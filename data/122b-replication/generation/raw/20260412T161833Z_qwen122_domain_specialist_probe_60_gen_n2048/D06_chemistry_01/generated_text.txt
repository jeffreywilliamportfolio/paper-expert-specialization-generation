Chemical bonding is the force that holds atoms together to form molecules, compounds, or extended structures. While the specific mechanisms differ, the underlying driver for all three types is the tendency of atoms to achieve a more stable electron configuration, often resembling that of a noble gas (the **octet rule**).

Here is how ionic, covalent, and metallic bonding work:

### 1. Ionic Bonding: The Electrostatic Attraction
Ionic bonding typically occurs between a **metal** and a **non-metal**. It is driven by the complete transfer of electrons.

*   **The Mechanism**: One atom (usually the metal) has a low ionization energy and readily loses one or more valence electrons to become a positively charged **cation**. The other atom (usually the non-metal) has a high electron affinity and gains those electrons to become a negatively charged **anion**.
*   **The Force**: Once the ions are formed, they are held together by strong **electrostatic forces** of attraction between the opposite charges.
*   **Structure**: Unlike molecules, ionic compounds form a giant **crystal lattice** structure where each ion is surrounded by ions of the opposite charge.
*   **Properties**: These compounds generally have high melting and boiling points, are brittle, and conduct electricity well when dissolved in water or melted (because the ions are free to move), but not when solid.
*   **Example**: Sodium Chloride ($NaCl$). Sodium ($Na$) loses an electron to become $Na^+$, and Chlorine ($Cl$) gains it to become $Cl^-$.

### 2. Covalent Bonding: The Sharing of Electrons
Covalent bonding typically occurs between two **non-metals**. It is driven by the sharing of electron pairs.

*   **The Mechanism**: Instead of transferring electrons, atoms share valence electrons to fill their outer shells. This sharing allows both atoms to achieve a stable octet.
*   **The Force**: The bond is formed by the electrostatic attraction between the **shared pair of electrons** (which are negatively charged) and the **nuclei** of the bonded atoms (which are positively charged).
*   **Types**:
    *   **Non-polar Covalent**: Electrons are shared equally (e.g., $O_2$).
    *   **Polar Covalent**: Electrons are shared unequally because one atom is more electronegative, creating a dipole (e.g., $H_2O$).
*   **Structure**: Covalent bonds form discrete **molecules**.
*   **Properties**: These substances often have lower melting/boiling points than ionic compounds, are poor conductors of electricity (as there are no free ions or electrons), and can exist as solids, liquids, or gases.
*   **Example**: Water ($H_2O$). Oxygen shares electrons with two Hydrogen atoms.

### 3. Metallic Bonding: The Sea of Electrons
Metallic bonding occurs between **metal atoms**. It is unique because it involves a lattice of positive ions immersed in a "sea" of delocalized electrons.

*   **The Mechanism**: Metal atoms have loosely held valence electrons. In a solid metal, these outer electrons detach from their parent atoms and become **delocalized**, meaning they are free to move throughout the entire structure. The metal atoms become positive ions (cations).
*   **The Force**: The bond is the strong electrostatic attraction between the **lattice of positive metal ions** and the **sea of delocalized electrons**.
*   **Structure**: A regular, closely packed **crystal lattice**.
*   **Properties**:
    *   **Conductivity**: The free-moving electrons allow metals to conduct heat and electricity efficiently.
    *   **Malleability/Ductility**: Because the ions are not locked in a rigid directional bond (like in ionic crystals), layers of atoms can slide over each other without breaking the bond.
    *   **Luster**: The interaction of light with the free electrons gives metals their shiny appearance.
*   **Example**: Copper ($Cu$) or Iron ($Fe$).

| Feature | Ionic Bonding | Covalent Bonding | Metallic Bonding |

| **Participants** | Metal + Non-metal | Non-metal + Non-metal | Metal + Metal |

| **Structure** | Crystal Lattice | Discrete Molecules | Crystal Lattice |

Chemical bonding is the fundamental force that holds atoms together, driven by the universal tendency of atoms to achieve a stable electron configuration (usually a full outer shell, known as the **octet rule**). While the goal is similar across all types, the mechanism of how electrons behave differs significantly between ionic, covalent, and metallic bonds.

Here is a breakdown of how each interaction works:

### 1. Ionic Bonding: The Transfer of Electrons
Ionic bonding typically occurs between a **metal** and a **non-metal**. It is an electrostatic attraction resulting from the complete transfer of electrons.

*   **The Process**:
    *   The metal atom (low electronegativity) loses one or more valence electrons to become a positively charged **cation**.
    *   The non-metal atom (high electronegativity) gains those electrons to become a negatively charged **anion**.
*   **The Bond**: The bond is the strong **electrostatic force** of attraction between the oppositely charged ions.
*   **Structure**: These ions arrange themselves into a giant, repeating 3D **crystal lattice** rather than forming discrete molecules.
*   **Key Properties**: High melting/boiling points, brittleness, and the ability to conduct electricity when dissolved in water or melted (because the ions are free to move).
*   **Example**: Sodium Chloride ($NaCl$). Sodium gives an electron to Chlorine, creating $Na^+$ and $Cl^-$, which stick together.

### 2. Covalent Bonding: The Sharing of Electrons
Covalent bonding usually happens between two **non-metals**. Instead of transferring electrons, atoms share them to fill their outer shells.

*   **The Process**:
    *   Two atoms approach each other, and their valence orbitals overlap.
    *   They share one or more pairs of electrons so that both atoms can effectively "count" those shared electrons toward their octet.
*   **The Bond**: The bond is the attraction between the **positively charged nuclei** of the atoms and the **negatively charged shared electron pair** located between them.
*   **Structure**: This forms discrete **molecules**.
*   **Variations**:
    *   *Non-polar*: Electrons are shared equally (e.g., $O_2$).
    *   *Polar*: Electrons are shared unequally due to differences in electronegativity, creating partial charges (e.g., $H_2O$).
*   **Key Properties**: Generally lower melting/boiling points than ionic compounds, poor electrical conductivity (no free ions/electrons), and can exist as solids, liquids, or gases.
*   **Example**: Water ($H_2O$). Oxygen shares electrons with two Hydrogen atoms.

### 3. Metallic Bonding: The "Sea of Electrons"
Metallic bonding occurs between **metal atoms**. It is unique because the electrons are not tied to any specific atom.

*   **The Process**:
    *   Metal atoms release their valence electrons, which become **delocalized**.
    *   These electrons form a mobile "sea" or "cloud" that flows freely throughout the entire structure.
    *   The metal atoms become fixed positive ions (cations) embedded in this electron sea.
*   **The Bond**: The bond is the strong electrostatic attraction between the **lattice of positive metal ions** and the **delocalized negative electrons**.
*   **Structure**: A closely packed **crystal lattice**.
*   **Key Properties**:
    *   **Conductivity**: The free-moving electrons allow for excellent conduction of heat and electricity.
    *   **Malleability & Ductility**: Layers of ions can slide past each other without breaking the bond (since the electron sea adjusts instantly), allowing metals to be hammered or drawn into wires.
    *   **Luster**: The interaction of light with the free electrons creates a shiny surface.
*   **Example**: Copper ($Cu$) or Iron ($Fe$).

| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Participants** | Metal + Non-metal | Non-metal + Non-metal | Metal + Metal |
